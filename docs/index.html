Top 10 Questions to Ask

Priority (Score 15+)

1. "Walk me through a typical work week — what does Monday through Friday look like?"
Owner described as sole officer but daily role is undisclosed. If they're the primary salesperson or customer relationship manager, transition risk is extreme. If they're primarily oversight and the GM runs operations, transition risk is manageable.
2. "The lease expires in about a year, with a two-year extension option. Have you had any conversations with the landlord about a longer-term lease? Would they be open to a 10-year extension?"
SBA 7(a) requires 10+ years of lease coverage. This is the #1 structural obstacle to financing the deal. If the landlord is unwilling or demands dramatically higher rent at market rate, the deal economics change fundamentally.
3. "The tax documents include a 2022 partnership return that ends in August, and the 2024 is an S-Corp. Can you walk through when the entity conversion happened and how the 2022 financials bridge between the two structures?"
CIM presents 2022 as a full-year figure without disclosing the short-year partnership. If this is ~8 months of data, the annualized figure is ~$2.5-2.8M and there's no revenue growth story. Also — where is the 2023 business tax return?
4. "Can you share a list of your top 10 customers and what percentage of revenue each represents?"
CIM claims "diversified base of repeat B2B customers" but provides zero concentration data. Nearly half of revenue appears to come from out-of-state sales — is that one large customer or many?
5. "The credit card balance is around ~$135K. What's typically funded through credit cards — is that seasonal inventory, equipment purchases, or operating cash flow timing?"
~$135K in CC debt at 14-19% interest is unusual for a business generating $250K+ in net income. Could indicate cash flow problems not visible in the P&L, or owner taking excess distributions and funding operations with debt.

Assessment

What this deal looks like: A solid, profitable commercial print shop generating ~$350-430K in verifiable SDE on $2.5-3M in stable (not growing) revenue. The business has real substance — 15-20 employees, decades-long track record, diverse B2B customer base, and a fully-equipped ~10,000 sq ft production facility.

What's genuinely good:

  • 2024 SDE independently confirmed at ~$430K — the seller's number checks out
  • SBA debt service coverage is strong (2.20x+)
  • Below-market lease is a current-period advantage
  • No debt to assume, clean legal history
  • Diversified service mix (offset + digital + large format + promo) hedges against commodity print decline

What's genuinely concerning:

  • The lease is a structural obstacle for SBA financing — resolving it may cost $35-75K/year in higher rent
  • Tax return documentation has significant gaps (short year, missing business return, entity conversion)
  • Revenue is flat, not growing — the CIM's growth narrative is misleading
  • All equipment at end of useful life with zero reinvestment
  • Owner's role and transition risk are completely unknown
  • Above-market asking multiple (2.9x) in a declining industry
Bottom line: This deal has real underlying value in the $900K-$1M range. At ~$1.0-1.2M, the buyer is paying a premium for a business in a declining industry with equipment risk, lease uncertainty, and documentation gaps. The SDE is real and verifiable for 2024-2025 — the seller isn't inflating the numbers. But the asking price doesn't account for post-acquisition reality (capex, lease renewal, replacement owner salary). This is worth pursuing at the right price, with the lease extension as the gating issue. If the landlord won't extend to 10 years at a reasonable rate, the SBA structure doesn't work and the deal economics change fundamentally.
