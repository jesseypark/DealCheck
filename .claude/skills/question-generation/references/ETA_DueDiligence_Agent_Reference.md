# Due Diligence Reference for ETA / IB Acquisition Review
### An agent-facing question bank and red-flag checklist

**Purpose.** This document is the knowledge base an AI agent should consult when helping a search-fund operator, ETA buyer, self-funded searcher, or investment banker evaluate a target company. It synthesizes seven source documents (Permanent Equity's *Do Diligence*, SMEVentures, Live Oak Bank, Entrepreneur & Innovation Exchange, the Initial DD Quick-Hit, VISO questionnaire, and a one-page DD checklist) into a single, prioritized reference.

**How the agent should use it.**
1. Treat each numbered prompt as a probe — ask it of the seller, the CIM, the data room, or the user.
2. For every section, surface the **red flags** listed at the end; if one is present, escalate and dig deeper rather than moving on.
3. When the user shares a data point (a multiple, a customer list, a P&L line), cross-reference it against the relevant section's questions and comment on what is missing, what is inconsistent, and what would change the decision.
4. Keep a running list of "items requiring verification" — this is the output the user can take to their CPA, attorney, and QofE provider.

**Priority tiering** — orange = frontload these; confusion here usually kills the deal.

---

## Section 0 — Deal-Breaker Screen (run first, always)

Before anything else, the agent should confirm the following. A "no" on any of these means the deal likely should not proceed to deeper diligence.

1. 🟠 **Seller motivation is coherent.** Why selling, why now, why to this buyer? Vague, shifting, or purely financial answers from a still-young owner are a flag.
2. 🟠 **Financials hang together.** Does revenue growth align with headcount and capacity? Do margins behave logically with volume? Does reported EBITDA convert to cash, or disappear into working capital and capex?
3. 🟠 **Access is open.** Seller willing to grant access to CFO, Head of Sales, key employees, and (eventually) top customers. Restricted access post-LOI is itself information — price it or walk.
4. 🟠 **Customer concentration is survivable.** Model the business with top 2 customers lost the day after close. If DSCR dies, the deal dies unless the price reflects it.
5. 🟠 **DSCR cushion exists at stressed inputs.** Plug in market-rate CEO salary, SBA-rate interest (11–12%), and still clear 1.25× — or the deal doesn't carry debt.
6. 🟠 **No prohibited-industry or compliance landmine.** Licenses transferable, no active environmental claims, no pending material litigation, no asbestos/OSHA/ICE/EEOC open matter that changes valuation.

If all six clear, proceed to the full checklist below.

## Section 1 — Seller & Transaction Readiness 🟠

*The Deeds article's core point: the best deal a client ever did was the one he walked away from. Seller psychology is the leading indicator.*

### 1.1 Motivation and commitment
1. Why is the seller selling? Why now? Why to this buyer specifically?
2. Has the seller tried to sell before? What happened and what changed?
3. How long has the seller considered selling? (Sudden sellers and years-long waverers are different risks.)
4. Does the seller understand what a seller note is, what cash-free/debt-free means, and how a working-capital peg works? If not, expect renegotiation drama near close.
5. Is the seller trustworthy and transparent in conversations so far? Any answers feel rehearsed?
6. Who is the seller's attorney — do they have real M&A experience, or will they relitigate every standard term?

### 1.2 Seller's exit feasibility
1. What is the seller's approximate net worth and how concentrated is it in this business?
2. Has the seller modeled their post-tax, post-seller-note take-home? (Sellers who haven't often pull out mid-deal.)
3. What are the seller's plans post-close? Where will they spend their time? Hobbies, next venture, retirement — all acceptable; "I'll figure it out" is not.
4. Is the seller willing to stay through a meaningful transition and **also** willing to leave at the end of it?

### 1.3 Key-person / owner entanglement
1. What does the owner actually do day-to-day — scheduling, sales, ops, key-customer babysitting? Price the replacement cost explicitly.
2. Does the brand or reputation ride on the owner's name? (Personal-brand businesses lose a chunk of value on sale.)
3. Are customer relationships institutional or personal to the owner?
4. Family members on payroll — who, doing what, staying or leaving, replacement cost?

### 1.4 Cap table and consents
1. Who are the other shareholders? Any silent partners, minority holders, or promised equity that could gum up the closing?
2. Any options, warrants, SARs, phantom equity, profits interests, or oral equity promises? (Permanent Equity flag.)
3. Voting agreements, ROFRs, co-sale rights, put/call arrangements?
4. Consents required from third parties (landlords, lenders, customers, governments, franchisors) — listed and timed?

**Red flags for this section:**
- Seller unwilling to leave after transition, or wants to retain material equity without clear strategic reason.
- Second failed sale process.
- Seller's attorney has never done an M&A deal.
- Owner still personally guarantees leases, loans, or customer contracts — unwinding these can kill timing.
- Key-person dependency > 30% of revenue or > 50% of key-customer relationships.

## Section 2 — Financial Diligence (Quality of Earnings) 🟠

*Normalization should make the deal harder to justify, not easier. If adjusted EBITDA keeps moving your way, you're fooling yourself.* — Deeds

### 2.1 Statement integrity
1. 3–5 years of monthly P&Ls, balance sheets, and cash-flow statements — audited, reviewed, compiled, or unaudited? (The further from audited, the more QofE matters.)
2. Interim monthly statements through the most recent month available.
3. 2+ years of bank statements — and have the buyer's CPA reconcile deposits to reported cash revenue. *Live Oak: the #1 source of post-close disputes is revenue misstatement; do this.*
4. Tax returns (federal, state, local, foreign) for the last five years. Pull IRS transcripts to confirm.
5. Accounting basis — cash or accrual? Any changes in accounting method or revenue-recognition policy in the last five years?
6. Are there any off-balance-sheet arrangements, side letters, handshake deals, or related-party transactions?

### 2.2 Revenue, margin, expense trend
1. Revenue trajectory — any one-time spikes/dips and the explanation for each. *"Spiked right before sale" is a specific risk Live Oak flags — sellers can pull revenue forward with discounts to juice the trailing number.*
2. Gross-margin stability — is COGS a steady % of sales? Explain every meaningful move.
3. Are margins trending down (masked by revenue growth)? Is the most recent period suspiciously the best? If so, value on a 3-year average, not TTM.
4. Controllable vs non-controllable expense trends over 5 years.
5. Does revenue recognition line up with cash? Any deferred revenue, billings in excess of costs, WIP overhang?
6. Has the seller "coasted to the finish line" — i.e., stopped selling — in the last 6–12 months? Check pipeline/backlog month-by-month.

### 2.3 EBITDA add-backs — the minefield
1. Is every add-back **quantifiable and verifiable** with a W-2, receipt, invoice, or signed agreement?
2. Owner comp replacement — what would a market-rate CEO cost to replace the seller's actual job? Use that number, not the seller's salary.
3. Discretionary expenses — vehicles, travel, meals, family payroll. Adjust only if you're sure they go away post-close.
4. "One-time" items — if a "one-time" legal matter, customer loss, or equipment failure has appeared every few years, it's not one-time. It's the business.
5. Rent normalization — if the seller owns the RE, price fair market rent into the pro forma, whether you buy the RE or not.
6. Personal expenses in COGS? These get missed more than you'd think.

### 2.4 Working capital and cash conversion
1. A/R aging — % over 90 days? >10% over 90 is a quality-of-customer-base flag (Live Oak).
2. A/P aging — has the seller stretched payables pre-close to puff up cash? (Classic trick.)
3. Inventory — obsolete, slow-moving, or dead stock hiding as an asset? Book-to-physical history?
4. Days sales in inventory / turnover by product line.
5. Working-capital peg — how is it defined in the LOI/APA? Target-level mechanic in place?
6. Seasonality — quarterly or monthly revenue pattern, and is there enough liquidity in the buyer's budget to bridge the trough after close?

### 2.5 Capital expenditure and maintenance
1. Historical capex spend vs. depreciation — underinvestment is a hidden earnings adjustment.
2. Deferred maintenance — equipment that "usually works," facility repairs, IT systems overdue for replacement. Not on the balance sheet but very real.
3. Near-term capex required: franchise remodels, equipment refresh cycles, compliance upgrades.
4. Replacement rate of fixed assets.

### 2.6 Taxes
1. Any federal, state, or local audits in the last 5 years? Results?
2. Sales-and-use tax — filed in every jurisdiction where nexus exists? (Wayfair exposure is a sneaky balance-sheet item.)
3. NOLs or credit carryforwards — and would a change of control wipe them out?
4. 338(h)(10) / F-reorg / asset-vs-stock structure — has this been modeled for after-tax price?
5. R&D credits, ERC, PPP forgiveness — all properly documented? Clawback risk?

**Red flags for this section:**
- Cash-basis books with large A/R or inventory (earnings are easily manipulated by timing).
- Large "one-time" expenses that recur.
- Add-backs > 25% of reported EBITDA without rock-solid documentation.
- Deposit-to-revenue reconciliation doesn't tie.
- Most recent year is dramatically better than prior three.
- Working-capital level has been shrinking in the months before sale.

## Section 3 — Customers & Revenue Quality 🟠

### 3.1 Concentration
1. Top 1, top 5, top 10 customers as % of revenue **and** % of gross profit (the gross-profit cut usually looks worse).
2. Run pro-forma cash flow with the top 2 customers lost day-after-close. Does DSCR hold?
3. Any related-party customers? (E.g., customer is also an investor, family member, or the owner's other business.)
4. Live Oak deal-killer test: if top 3 > 35% of revenue, consider a seller note with forgiveness tied to retention of those specific accounts.

### 3.2 Retention and stickiness
1. Top-10 customer revenue year-by-year for 3 years — are the same names there? Growing, flat, declining?
2. Customer tenure distribution — median and long-tail.
3. Contracted vs. at-will revenue — how much revenue sits under written contracts with term > 12 months? Auto-renew provisions?
4. Historical churn — both logo churn and revenue churn.
5. Why do customers leave? Interview 2–3 former customers if possible.
6. What are customer switching costs — real ones, not theoretical?

### 3.3 Customer voice (insist on calls)
1. Why do you buy from this company?
2. Are you loyal to the business or to the owner personally?
3. How likely are you to continue buying at the current volume?
4. Who is your primary point of contact, and how do you rate them?
5. NPS-style: how likely to refer?
6. What would make you leave?

### 3.4 Pricing power and unit economics
1. History of price increases — frequency, magnitude, customer reaction.
2. How is price set: cost-plus or willingness-to-pay? (Cost-plus shops rarely have real pricing power.)
3. Can the business pass through commodity, labor, FX, or tariff inflation? Any contractual index clauses?
4. CAC / LTV if the business has enough data; if not, at least directional unit economics per channel.

### 3.5 Pipeline, backlog, seasonality
1. Backlog and WIP schedule — customer, size, start date, % complete, costs in excess of billings and vice versa.
2. Monthly revenue pattern — seasonal, cyclical, event-driven?
3. Recession test: how did this business perform 2008–2010 and 2020? If no data, what's the cyclicality of the customer base? (Live Oak outright declines some industries for this reason.)
4. Pipeline trend into close — flat/growing = fine; sharp decline = seller coasting.

### 3.6 Discounts, rebates, warranties
1. Any discount / rebate / credit programs, and are they booked correctly (net revenue, not contra-expense)?
2. Warranty obligations, historical claims, and reserve adequacy.
3. Return rates, refund policies.

**Red flags:**
- Top customer > 20% of revenue AND owner-held relationship AND no contract.
- Revenue spike in the trailing 6–12 months that can't be traced to a specific, durable driver.
- Top-10 list shows high churn year-over-year.
- No customer contracts, or contracts expire within 90 days of close.
- Seller resists customer calls or insists on being present.

## Section 4 — Suppliers, Inventory, and Cost Base

1. Top 10 suppliers by spend, with % of COGS and single-source flags.
2. Contract terms — pricing, exclusivity, MFN, auto-renew, change-of-control triggers.
3. Replaceability — if each top supplier disappeared, what's the switch cost and lead time?
4. Are any suppliers also owed back payments that could trigger a supply halt at close?
5. Commodity / FX / tariff exposure and any hedging in place.
6. Inventory: current level, aged categories, book-to-physical variance history, turnover by SKU/product line.
7. Any supply disruption in the last 3 years that affected revenue? How was it resolved?
8. Have any suppliers *recently* stopped doing business with the company? Ask why, and call them.

**Red flags:** sole-source critical input, supplier also a competitor, >90-day A/P stretch on key vendors, inventory not reconciled in >12 months.

---

## Section 5 — Employees, Management, and Culture 🟠

### 5.1 Org and census
1. Full employee schedule: name, title, start date, location, comp (base + variable), benefits, W-2 vs. 1099.
2. Org chart with reporting lines.
3. 1099 classification — are contractors actually contractors under IRS and state-law tests? (Common hidden liability.)
4. Any staffing-agency or PEO arrangements, and who's on whose books?
5. Immigrant-labor exposure, I-9 practices, any ICE activity.

### 5.2 Key employees
1. Who runs the business if the owner walks out tomorrow?
2. For each key person: tenure, experience, age, comp, likely retention post-close, replacement difficulty.
3. Do the key people know a sale is happening? If not, what's the plan to tell them and when?
4. Restrictive covenants — non-competes, non-solicits, confidentiality — in place and enforceable in the relevant state? (California and a growing list of states don't enforce non-competes.)
5. Any pending retirements, recent resignations, or firings of senior staff?
6. Stay bonuses or equity carrots proposed for GMs/key ops leaders?

### 5.3 Comp, benefits, ERISA
1. Exec comp schedule for last 3 years (salary + bonus + non-cash).
2. Benefit plans list: 401(k), health, dental, LTD, profit-sharing, deferred comp, phantom equity.
3. Plan docs, Form 5500s, IRS determination letters, any pending compliance issues.
4. Unfunded pension liabilities or multi-employer plan withdrawal liability — **this can exceed the purchase price**.
5. Accrued PTO liability.
6. Deferred comp plans — who's owed what, and when does it become due?

### 5.4 Labor and employment risk
1. Union representation — current CBAs, any organizing activity?
2. Any current or past EEOC, DOL, OSHA, NLRB, or ICE actions in last 5 years?
3. Pending discrimination, harassment, retaliation, or wage-hour claims.
4. Workers' comp loss history and mod rate.
5. Severance obligations triggered by change of control.

### 5.5 Culture and tenure
1. Retention rate, by function, over 3 years.
2. Why do people join, stay, and leave? (Ask employees directly if granted access.)
3. Wage-scale vs. local market — is the company a low-payer riding on loyalty? That ends fast with a new owner.
4. What's the mission/values statement — and do employees actually know it?

**Red flags:**
- Ops run entirely out of the owner's head.
- Key employee recently departed "to pursue other opportunities."
- 1099 workforce doing core work.
- No I-9s on file.
- Pension with unfunded liability.
- Non-competes in a non-compete-unfriendly state and the plan depends on them.

## Section 6 — Assets, Equipment, Real Estate

1. Fixed-asset schedule — owned vs. leased, cost, accumulated depreciation, insured value, estimated remaining useful life, condition, appraised value.
2. Physical walk-through — inspect every major piece of equipment, don't take the schedule on faith. (Live Oak case: X-ray machine non-functional; CNC machines water-damaged pre-close.)
3. Recent third-party equipment inspection or appraisal.
4. Rolling stock / vehicles — state of registration, titles clean, transfer mechanics.
5. Are any "company" assets actually held in a related entity the seller keeps? (Live Oak case: landscaping company where equipment sat in a separate LLC.)
6. Upcoming replacement capex in the next 24 months.
7. Real estate owned: address, title, mortgages, liens, environmental history, fair market rent, option to buy.
8. Real estate leased: term remaining, extension options (SBA wants 10 years total), renewal rent mechanic, market-rent gap, personal guarantees, assignment/change-of-control clauses, landlord consent required at close.
9. Certificates of occupancy, zoning compliance, use permits.
10. Encumbrances — liens, easements, UCC filings. Run a UCC search.

**Red flags:** deferred maintenance not on balance sheet, lease expiring within 24 months, owner-held RE with above-market rent being imposed as part of the deal.

---

## Section 7 — Intellectual Property, Technology, and Data

1. Inventory of all IP: patents, trademarks, copyrights, domains, trade secrets, custom software — owned vs. licensed.
2. For every patent/trademark: jurisdiction, registration number, owner of record, renewal dates.
3. Invention-assignment and work-for-hire agreements from every employee and contractor who's touched the IP. Missing ones = ownership gap.
4. Open-source usage — any copyleft licenses (GPL) embedded in shipped code?
5. Third-party IP licenses — transferable? Change-of-control triggers?
6. Pending or threatened IP disputes, infringement claims, or demand letters.
7. Software and systems: core applications, custom code, hosting, backups, disaster recovery, SLAs.
8. License compliance — are you paying for the right number of seats?
9. Cybersecurity — breaches in last 5 years, remediation, current posture, cyber-insurance coverage.
10. Data privacy — what PII is collected, what policies exist, GDPR/CCPA/HIPAA exposure, DPAs with vendors.
11. Website and social media — ownership of accounts, transfer mechanics, influencer or content-creator agreements.

**Red flags:** key software written by a contractor with no IP assignment, a pending infringement letter not disclosed, unlicensed commercial software, no written privacy policy in a business handling consumer PII.

---

## Section 8 — Legal, Litigation, Regulatory, Environmental

1. Litigation past 5 years: pleadings, settlements, judgments, counsel letters.
2. Pending litigation or regulatory proceedings — and counsel's view of exposure.
3. Threatened litigation — demand letters, hostile counterparty correspondence.
4. Consent decrees, injunctions, or outstanding judgments against the company or any officer.
5. Criminal matters involving any officer, director, or 10%+ owner.
6. Permits and licenses — every one required to operate, expiration dates, transferability on change of control.
7. Government contracts — assignment rules (often require novation), small-business set-aside status that may not transfer.
8. Export/import: BIS, OFAC, DDTC compliance; any denied parties in the customer list.
9. Environmental: current and prior facilities, Phase I (and Phase II if indicated), hazardous-material usage, RCRA/CERCLA exposure, asbestos, underground storage tanks.
10. Industry-specific regulation: healthcare (HIPAA, Stark, kickback), financial services, food, cannabis, childcare, etc. — which agencies and what's the current compliance posture?
11. Franchise agreements (if franchisee): transfer approval, remodel obligations, territory rights, royalty/ad-fund structure.

**Red flags:** open environmental Notice of Violation, permit that doesn't transfer on change of control, a pending class action, DOJ/SEC inquiry of any kind.

---

## Section 9 — Debt, Obligations, and Off-Balance-Sheet

1. All outstanding debt: short-term, long-term, intercompany, shareholder loans. Interest rate, maturity, prepayment, change-of-control covenants.
2. Letters of credit, performance bonds, guarantees — given and received.
3. Capital leases, synthetic leases, sale-leasebacks, installment purchases.
4. UCC-1 filings against company assets — clean at close?
5. Any loans between the company and officers, family, or employees?
6. Earn-outs, contingent consideration, or deferred payments from prior acquisitions.
7. Unpaid dividends or distributions owed.
8. Stock buyback obligations.
9. Indemnification obligations from prior transactions.

**Red flags:** personal guarantees that can't be released at close, change-of-control acceleration clauses in material debt, undisclosed loans from the company to the owner or family.

---

## Section 10 — Insurance and Bonding

1. Policy schedule: GL, property, auto, WC, umbrella, D&O, E&O, cyber, EPL, key-person, product liability, environmental.
2. 5-year loss history, pending claims, cancellations, denials.
3. Deductibles, coverage limits, any self-insured retention.
4. Is coverage adequate for the industry and deal structure? (New owner often needs new limits, new quotes.)
5. Bonding capacity and current bonds (construction/contractor businesses).
6. Is any policy going to terminate on change of control?

**Red flags:** history of claim denials, a canceled policy that hasn't been re-placed, WC mod rate significantly above 1.0.

---

## Section 11 — Industry, Market, and Competitive Position

1. Market size in the target geography; growth rate and the real driver behind it.
2. Competitive set — how many, how concentrated, who's winning, why.
3. Company's differentiation — and is it actually real, or is it "we care more"?
4. Barriers to entry — real ones (regulation, scale, specialized labor, certifications) vs. imagined ones.
5. Substitutes and technological displacement risk (AI, automation, direct-to-consumer, etc.).
6. Recent M&A in the sector, multiples paid, who's buying, who's selling, who's consolidating.
7. Who are the natural exit buyers in 5–7 years? PE rollups, strategics, both? If nobody, pricing and hold horizon need to reflect that.
8. Regulatory trajectory — getting tighter, looser, changing?

**Red flags:** buyer can't name 3 credible exit candidates, technological substitute is already eating a competitor's lunch, regulatory tailwind ending within the hold period.

---

## Section 12 — Deal Structure, Financing, and Closing Mechanics 🟠

1. Purchase-price multiple vs. sector comps — and weighted toward most-recent year or a 3-year average? Weight declining trends heavier.
2. Stock vs. asset deal — implications for liabilities assumed, step-up, and seller tax.
3. Working-capital peg — definition, target level, true-up mechanic, caps on variance.
4. Cash-free / debt-free — explicitly agreed and defined in the LOI.
5. Seller financing — size, interest rate, amortization, standby to senior, forgiveness triggers tied to customer retention or earnout performance (Live Oak recommends this for concentrated deals).
6. Equity rollover — size, class, rights, exit mechanism, tag-along, drag-along.
7. Earnout, if any — metric, formula, measurement period, dispute mechanic, acceleration on termination.
8. Reps, warranties, indemnification — survival period, baskets, caps, escrow or RWI.
9. DSCR stress test — market-rate CEO salary, SBA-rate interest (assume 11–12%), still > 1.25×?
10. Consent list — every third party whose signature is required to close, with status.
11. Closing conditions — especially any customer/employee retention targets.
12. Transaction expenses — legal, QofE, environmental, reps-and-warranties insurance — budgeted and who pays at close?

**Red flags:** LOI silent on working-capital peg, seller wants "cash at close + A/R retained" (that's effectively a price hike), personal guarantees on existing debt that can't be released, consents from a key customer required at close but not yet approached.

## Section 13 — Post-Close / Day-1 Plan (diligence should pressure-test this)

1. Who is the new CEO day one, and do they have the domain skills? (Deeds: "ETA is not the time to reinvent yourself.")
2. First-90-day plan — three things changed, three things explicitly left alone.
3. How will the owner be introduced out, and to whom, on what timeline?
4. Retention plan for the top 5 customers and top 5 employees.
5. Licensing / certification transfers — who needs to hold what, and is the buyer ready?
6. Cash runway — working capital + revolver for the first 6–12 months including any revenue trough.
7. Systems transitions — accounting, payroll, banking, insurance, email, domains.
8. Communication plan: employees, customers, suppliers, community. Sequence matters.

**Red flags:** buyer has no written 100-day plan before closing, no plan for the seller's replacement in critical functions, no bridge liquidity modeled.

---

## Section 14 — "What Else?" Catch-Alls

*Every business hides something in an odd place. These are the final sweep questions.*

1. Any oral agreements, side letters, or handshake deals — with customers, suppliers, employees, landlords, regulators?
2. Any material commitments made in the last 90 days (pricing guarantees, rebates, contract renewals) that a buyer should know about?
3. Any relationships between the business and the owner personally that evaporate on close (e.g., truck "owned" by the company but titled to the owner)?
4. Any "off the books" revenue or expenses — cash sales, undeclared tips, personal expenses running through the P&L?
5. Related-party rent, services, or purchases — documented and at market?
6. Any standalone IP, brand, domain, or social account held personally by the seller that the business depends on?
7. Is there anything the seller would want to know if the roles were reversed?

## Agent Behavior — How to Use This Reference

When a user drops a CIM, data-room document, seller email, or financial statement into the conversation, the agent should:

1. **Map the content to sections above.** If the user shares a P&L, go to Section 2. If they share a customer list, go to Section 3.
2. **Score the data against the relevant red flags.** Explicitly call out matches.
3. **Surface missing items.** The agent should never just summarize what's in front of it — it should also list what's *not* there but should be.
4. **Frame findings as questions to the seller**, not conclusions. "This is what I'd ask the seller:" is more useful than "This is bad."
5. **Recommend specialists.** QofE to a CPA, employment law to counsel, environmental to a Phase I provider, IT to a tech DD consultant. The agent is a quarterback, not a replacement for those roles.
6. **Flag the "opacity tax."** Any section where access is denied or information is missing should increase the implied risk discount in the valuation. Deeds: "Unknowns do not disappear because they are inconvenient. They surface later, usually after closing, when your leverage is gone."
7. **Keep a running issue log.** Output at the end of any substantive review: (a) confirmed facts, (b) open questions, (c) red flags, (d) price/structure implications.

### Tone and stance
- Skeptical, not cynical. Sellers are not adversaries; they are counterparties with different information.
- Quantify when possible. "Customer concentration is high" is less useful than "Top 3 = 47% of GP; losing #1 collapses DSCR to 0.9×."
- Prefer paraphrased, plain-language questions the user can copy-paste into a seller call.
- Default to "what would change my mind about this deal?" over "does this deal work?"

### Standard output format after reviewing a document

```
SUMMARY: [2-3 sentences on what the document says]

CONFIRMED: [bullet list of facts this document establishes]

OPEN QUESTIONS: [bullet list of things the agent would ask the seller next]

RED FLAGS: [bullet list, each mapped to a section above]

PRICE / STRUCTURE IMPLICATIONS: [if any finding would change the offer, escrow, seller note, or walk-away]

NEXT DOCUMENTS TO REQUEST: [concrete data-room items]
```

## Appendix A — Document Request List (Day-One Data Room Ask)

From the Live Oak / Permanent Equity / SMEVentures overlap, these are the documents to request immediately after LOI:

**Financial**
- 3–5 years of annual P&L, balance sheet, cash flow
- Monthly P&L and BS for trailing 24 months
- 3–5 years of federal and state tax returns
- 2 years of bank statements, all accounts
- A/R and A/P aging as of most recent month-end
- Inventory schedule and aging
- Fixed-asset schedule with depreciation policy
- Historical capex and projected capex
- Add-back schedule with line-item support
- Budget vs. actual for last 3 years
- Current-year budget and 3-year projection with assumptions

**Customer / Revenue**
- Top-20 customers by revenue for each of last 3 years
- Monthly revenue by customer for trailing 24 months
- Customer contract copies for top 20
- Pipeline / backlog / WIP report
- Churn analysis
- Price list and discount/rebate programs

**Supplier / Cost**
- Top-10 supplier list with spend
- Key supplier contracts
- Commodity / input-cost history if relevant

**People**
- Employee census (name, title, tenure, comp, benefits)
- Org chart
- Key-employee agreements and any restrictive covenants
- Benefit plan documents + Form 5500s
- WC loss runs (5 years)
- Any pending or closed employment claims

**Legal / Corporate**
- Articles, bylaws, operating agreements, cap table
- Minute book if available
- All material contracts (customer, supplier, lease, license, debt)
- All litigation files for last 5 years
- Permits, licenses, certifications
- Insurance schedule + 5-year loss history

**Real Estate / Environmental**
- Owned-property deeds, surveys, title policies
- Leases, subleases, estoppels
- Prior environmental reports (Phase I, Phase II, tank testing)

**IP / Tech**
- IP schedule (patents, marks, copyrights, domains)
- Material software licenses
- Cyber-incident history

## Appendix B — Industry-Specific Probes to Layer On

**Construction / contractors:** bonding capacity, WIP schedule, % complete accounting, multiemployer pension withdrawal liability, licensing in every state of operation, OSHA loss history.

**Healthcare / dental / vet:** provider credentialing, payer-mix concentration, Stark/anti-kickback, HIPAA, state licensure of owner.

**Manufacturing:** equipment condition, environmental (RCRA, TSCA, asbestos), OSHA recordables, customer-specific tooling ownership.

**Distribution / wholesale:** supplier exclusivity and territory rights, rebate programs (MDF, SPIFFs), consignment inventory.

**Food service / restaurants:** health-code history, franchise remodel obligations, liquor license transferability, lease-assignment clauses, labor turnover norms.

**Software / SaaS:** ARR vs. revenue, net revenue retention, churn, usage-based vs. seat pricing, open-source compliance, data-privacy posture.

**Professional services:** owner-as-rainmaker risk, non-compete enforceability in the operating state, client consent to assignment of engagement letters.

**Franchises / multi-unit:** franchise-agreement transfer approval, personal-guaranty release, territory rights, area-development commitments, remodel-due dates.

---

*Last principle (Deeds): "No amount of diligence eliminates uncertainty. At some point, the question becomes whether you understand the business well enough to live with its risks. Walking away from a deal is not failure. It is judgment."*
